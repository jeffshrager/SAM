### TN-001 — How the model is actually trained on input→output pairs

**Question:** Is the model trained on the entire string `"this->that"`, or is it
trained that `"this"` being input produces `"that"` as output?

**Answer:** Both are true simultaneously, and the distinction matters.

The full string `"this->that"` is fed in as a single token sequence. The model
predicts the next token at every position, but the loss mask zeros out everything
except the answer side (after `>`). So:

- The model **sees** all of `"this->"` as context when predicting `"that"` —
  the input side is not discarded or encoded separately.
- The model only receives **gradient signal** for predicting the `"that"` tokens.
- The `"this"` tokens are fully processed through all transformer layers and
  shape the hidden state that produces the answer; they just don't contribute
  to the loss.

This is the standard **decoder-only (GPT-style)** approach, as opposed to an
**encoder-decoder** architecture where `"this"` would be encoded into a fixed
vector and `"that"` decoded from that encoding. Here everything is one unified
causal sequence — the question side is read for free as context.

**Practical implication:** At inference time, `"this->"` is fed as the prompt
and the model continues generating `"that"` autoregressively, one token at a
time. This is exactly what `greedy_decode()` in `train.py` does. The model was
trained in exactly that condition — the only difference during training is that
the gold answer tokens are present in the sequence (teacher forcing), whereas
at inference they are generated step by step from the model's own predictions.

**Why this matters for the research:**
The model has full visibility into the structure of `"this"` when producing
each token of `"that"`. Whether it exploits that structure (e.g. attending back
to sign-flipping operators in the input) or learns shallow positional heuristics
is an open empirical question — and a prime target for mechanistic probes
(attention visualization, activation patching).

### TN-002 — Training data realism
*(Prompted by David's comments, 20260429_Discussion.txt)*

The current data generator produces expressions that are syntactically complex
in a way that doesn't match human exposure to mathematics. Real human math
experience is characterized by:
- Mostly 2-3 term expressions; 4-5 terms occasional; more than 5 very rare
- Variation primarily in *numbers and operations*, not syntactic depth
- Frank repetition of common forms

The current generator varies *syntactic depth* as its primary axis, which
produces expressions a human learner would essentially never encounter.

**Implication:** This may affect what representations the model develops and
whether those representations are meaningfully comparable to human cognition.
It is less critical for the current negation-distribution task (which is
deliberately abstract) but becomes important once we move to arithmetic or
equivalence understanding tasks intended to model human development.

**Status:** Flagged for team discussion before any changes are made.

### TN-003 — `->` as a literal symbol; the `=` vs `->` confusion
*(Prompted by David's comments, 20260429_Discussion.txt)*

**Key insight (David):** Since `->` is literally present in the token sequence
(it is not an architectural abstraction — the model sees `-` and `>` as
tokens), the model could in principle learn the *relationship* between `=` and
`->` if both appear in training data. This is architecturally different from
what one might naively assume.

**The children's `=` misconception:** Children systematically misread `=` in
expressions like `3+4+1 = 3+_` as meaning "compute the left side" (giving `8`
or `11`) rather than reading it relationally (giving `5`). This happens because
children are trained almost exclusively on `=` in the context `expression = answer`,
where `=` functions operationally. The model may replicate this exact error for
the same structural reason — if `=` appears only in operational contexts during
training, the model will have learned that meaning of `=`.

**Proposed experiment** *(do not implement until team discussion)*:
1. Add `=` and `_` to the vocabulary.
2. Train on a mix that includes both:
   - Current operational form: `3+2 -> 5`
   - Relational form: `3+4+1 = 3+_` (with answer `5`)
3. Test whether the model answers `8`/`11` (operational misreading) or `5`
   (relational understanding) on held-out equivalence trials.
4. Vary the proportion of relational vs. operational training examples to see
   if and when the correct relational interpretation emerges.

This is low-hanging fruit: small vocab change, new data generator branch,
testable with existing infrastructure. It directly operationalizes one of the
central research questions about equivalence understanding.

**Status:** Flagged for team discussion before implementation.

### TN-005 — `->` is two tokens, not one; replace with a single separator symbol

`->` is not a single token — the model sees `-` followed by `>`, two symbols
that already have meaning elsewhere in the vocabulary (`-` is subtraction and
negation; `>` appears only as the second half of `->` and was a deliberate
workaround to avoid collision).  This is architecturally untidy:

- The model cannot perceive `->` as a unified "transform" operator.
- `-` already carries two meanings in expressions (subtraction, negation);
  giving it a third role as the start of the separator is a source of
  potential confusion in the learned representations.
- The "everything before this token is input, everything after is output"
  boundary is harder to learn when the boundary marker is two tokens that
  individually mean other things.

**Proposed fix:** Replace `->` with a single unambiguous separator character
not otherwise in the vocabulary — e.g. `|` or `~`.  This is a low-cost
cleanup: one-character change to `VOCAB` and `make_pair()` in `data_gen.py`,
update `SEP_ID` in `train.py`, regenerate data.  No architectural changes.

This also makes the relationship between `=` and the separator cleaner for
TN-003: both would be single, unambiguous tokens whose learned meanings can be
directly compared.

**Status:** Concrete cleanup — recommend doing this before the next round of
experiments.  Flagged for team sign-off on choice of separator character.

### TN-004 — Arithmetic vs. pure symbol manipulation; the number line hypothesis
*(Prompted by Jeff's comments, 20260429_Discussion.txt)*

The current model performs only *symbol manipulation* — it rearranges tokens
without ever evaluating numerical expressions. Jeff raises the question of
whether the model should actually compute (e.g. `3+2 -> 5`) rather than only
redistribute signs.

**The deeper question:** If a model is trained on mathematics as a *pure symbol
manipulation game* — never given numerical magnitudes, never told that `5 > 3`,
never shown a number line — will it nonetheless develop internal representations
that are functionally equivalent to numerical magnitude? I.e., will something
like a number line emerge implicitly from the structure of symbolic relationships?

This is one of the central research questions of the project. Three conditions
worth eventually comparing:
1. **Pure symbol manipulation** — current setup; no arithmetic evaluation
2. **Symbol manipulation + arithmetic** — expressions evaluate to results;
   the model sees numerical outcomes but is not given magnitude structure
3. **Grounded** — the model is explicitly given a number line or magnitude
   representation as part of its input

The prediction is that (3) gives the best generalization, (2) intermediate,
and (1) worst — but the interesting question is *how much* (1) falls short,
and whether any magnitude-like structure emerges at all in condition (1).

**Mechanistic probe target:** Once a model reaches high accuracy (>50%
test_deeper), probe the residual stream for linear representations of numerical
magnitude. If a number line exists implicitly, it should be decodable from
intermediate activations even in condition (1).

**Status:** Long-term research direction; flagged for team discussion.
No implementation until team agrees on sequencing relative to other experiments.
