Project Outline: NanoGPT Educational Models for Studying "Understanding" in Tiny Transformers
1. Project Overview & Motivation

Core Goal: Build and study minimal, from-scratch GPT-style transformers (nano-LLMs) that train quickly on a laptop (~minutes) in pure Python/PyTorch.
Domain: Controlled symbolic algebra on single-digit numbers (to avoid tokenization issues).
Input: Arithmetic expressions (e.g., -(a+b), fractions, division).
Task: Next-token prediction to generate rewritten/normalized outputs (e.g., distribute negation, simplify, evaluate).

Scientific Aim: Use the fully synthetic, inspectable environment as a clean lab to probe what "understanding" means in LLMs — beyond surface patterns toward stable, reusable, compositional transformations.
Key Strengths: Tiny scale enables mechanistic interpretability, curriculum experiments, and causal claims impossible at full scale.

2. Core Experimental Setup

Data Generation:
Simple grammar for expressions (digits, +, -, parentheses, unary minus, later fractions/division).
Explicit symbolic rewriter produces canonical targets (e.g., reduced fractions, negation distribution: -(3+4) → -3-4).
High-level pseudocode structure for dataset generation and rewriting.

Model:
Tiny decoder-only transformer (1–2 layers, small embedding/d_model, symbol-level vocab).
Standard next-token prediction training.

Training Regimes (central axis):
Joint/Random: All expression types mixed from the start.
Progressive/Curriculum: Start tiny (digits) → add operations incrementally (addition, signed, fractions, division, rewrites).


3. Key Extensions & Probes

Fractions & Division:
Canonical reduced fractions.
Probe inversion (division = multiplication by inverse) and normalization.

Name Hints / Labels:
Symbolic labels (e.g., INVERT-MULTIPLY: a/b → a*(1/b)).
Test if stable labels aid task partitioning, subproblem organization, and generalization (no real-world semantics needed).

Evaluation Focus:
Generalization: Held-out digits, deeper nesting, recombined structures, OOD compositions.
Metrics: Accuracy + behavioral signatures of understanding (compositional transfer, invariance under equivalence).


4. Research Questions (from David’s interests)

Reification:
Transition from implicit procedural competence (e.g., commuting 1+9 → 9+1) to explicit representation/reasoning about concepts (e.g., "is addition commutative?").
Does labeling accelerate reification? Does reification improve downstream learning/problem-solving?
Modeling conceptual ↔ procedural knowledge interaction.

Misconceptions:
Induce via biased/incomplete training data (e.g., only smaller-from-larger subtraction).
Distinguish procedural errors vs. coherent incorrect internal "understandings."
Use internals (activations, attention, probes) + behavioral consistency tests.

Emergence of Algebraic Reasoning:
General principles over classes (e.g., n+n = 2*n, product of integers >1 is larger).
When/how does it emerge? Role of curriculum? Representation in model?


5. Broader Framing & Operational Definition of "Understanding"

Behavioral: Stable, reusable, compositional transformations that generalize under shifts (depth, digits, recombinations, equivalences).
Mechanistic: Recoverable latent intermediates, structured representations, strategy switching (e.g., via entropy/confidence), sensitivity to labels/curriculum.
Not Just Symbolic vs. Statistical: Focus on developmental order effects, internal organization, and parallels to human cognitive phenomena.

6. Feasibility & Next Steps

Fully laptop-trainable in minutes; inspectable internals.
High feasibility for all directions; misconceptions easiest, reification most novel, algebraic emergence hardest but high-impact.
Potential outputs: One-page research note (already drafted), minimal PyTorch script + dataset generator, interpretability probes.
Future extensions: RLHF toy versions, SMM-style confidence/fallback strategies, etc.

1. Scaling to 2- and 3-digit numbers on a dedicated 24GB RTX 3090: feasibility and rough resource estimate
Short answer: Yes — easily feasible, with room to spare.
The current single-digit setup uses a tiny model (typically 1–10M parameters, 1–6 layers, d_model ~64–384, tiny vocab of ~20–30 symbols, short sequences ~20–100 tokens). These run in minutes even on CPU or a laptop GPU.
Switching to 2–3 digit numbers (still character/symbol-level tokenization to avoid real-LLM tokenization artifacts) mainly increases:

Sequence length (roughly 2–3× longer for equivalent expression depth/complexity).
Dataset size needed for coverage (combinatorial explosion of possible numbers).

Rough resource calc (based on NanoGPT benchmarks and standard transformer memory formulas):

Model size: Still tiny. Even bumping to ~25–50M params (for headroom) uses ~0.2 GB (FP16) just for weights.
Training memory (parameters + gradients + optimizer states + activations): Rule of thumb is 4–18× model size depending on precision/optimizer. With activations scaling ~linearly in batch × seq_len × d_model (and attention quadratic in seq_len), a realistic config (seq_len=256, batch=32–128, FP16) stays well under 2–5 GB VRAM.
3090 (24 GB): You could comfortably run much larger experiments — e.g., deeper models, bigger batches, longer sequences (up to 512+), or even 4–5 digit numbers before hitting limits. Gradient checkpointing or smaller batches would give extra margin if needed.
Training time: Still minutes to a few hours per full run (depending on total tokens/epochs). Synthetic data can be generated on-the-fly, so no I/O bottleneck.

Bottom line: the 3090 gives us plenty of headroom to explore multi-digit arithmetic while keeping everything inspectable and fast. We can start with 2-digit and scale up incrementally.
2. Testing generalization to held-out digits: implementation details
This is a classic (and strong) test of systematic extrapolation / true rule learning, but we have to be precise about what “held-out” means.
Implementation:

Fix the full vocabulary upfront (all digits 0–9 + operators, parentheses, etc.).
In the dataset generator, create separate digit pools:
Training: sample expressions using only a subset (e.g., digits 0–4 or 0–7).
Test: include held-out digits (e.g., 5–9) in novel expressions.

The model therefore has an embedding vector for every digit from the very beginning, but some digits never appear in any training context. It must extrapolate their “role” purely from the abstract patterns learned on the seen digits.

This is not impossible — small transformers do show some success on this kind of compositional generalization in arithmetic papers (they learn something like “digit tokens are interchangeable placeholders once the rewrite rule is internalized”). Success here would be strong evidence of genuine symbolic-style understanding rather than surface memorization.
If the model fails badly on truly unseen tokens (which is common at very small scale), we can fall back to “held-out combinations” (all digits seen, but never certain pairings or depths) — still a very powerful test. Either way, it’s trivial to implement in the data generator and gives clean train/test splits.
3. New combinations of learned symbols/representations as a test of generalizability
I think this is elegant, powerful, and exactly the right framing.
It directly operationalizes compositional generalization / systematicity (the classic Fodor/Pylyshyn test): the model is trained on certain building blocks (e.g., -(a+b), (a+(b+c))) but tested on novel recombinations (-(a+(b+c))). Success means it has learned reusable primitives and composition rules rather than rote patterns.
This is:

Cleaner than pure held-out digits (avoids the “never-seen-token” edge case).
Highly aligned with cognitive-science benchmarks for reasoning.
Easy to generate and measure (accuracy on recombined vs. familiar splits).
Directly addresses your interest in “true reasoning abilities.”

It pairs beautifully with the curriculum axis (progressive vs. joint) — we can ask whether staged training leads to better compositional behavior. Papers on small transformers confirm this is one of the most revealing probes for whether models move beyond statistical interpolation.
4. Testing bounded rationality (Herbert Simon) in the current experimental setup
Yes — this nano setup is an ideal laboratory for it.
Simon’s bounded rationality: agents have limited information, time, and computational resources, so they satisfice rather than optimize perfectly. They develop efficient internal representations, heuristics, and strategies that let them “punch above their weight” — solving complex problems with limited resources by compressing the environment and reusing general patterns.
In our tiny transformer:

Limited resources are built-in: fixed (small) number of parameters/layers = strict computational bound.
Environment is fully controllable: we know the exact generative rules of the algebra world.

Concrete ways to test it:

Efficient representations: Use mechanistic interpretability (attention patterns, activation patching, probing) to check whether the model develops compact circuits that implement general rules (e.g., a reusable “distribute negation” subroutine) instead of memorizing every shallow case.
Heuristics / strategy switching: Look for SMM-style behavior — e.g., high-entropy / low-confidence cases trigger fallback strategies (re-evaluate step-by-step vs. direct rewrite).
Scaling behavior: Does performance degrade gracefully (or stay high) on deeper/more complex expressions, or does it collapse? Efficient bounded agents should generalize far beyond their training distribution using abstractions.
Curriculum effects: Progressive training may encourage the emergence of more human-like efficient representations (building abstractions bottom-up), while joint training might lead to more brittle, high-capacity memorization.
“Punching above weight” metric: Compare accuracy vs. effective compute (e.g., does a 2-layer model solve 5-deep nesting after learning the right abstractions?).

This directly connects to your reification and algebraic-reasoning questions: reification is precisely the process of turning implicit procedures into explicit, reusable concepts that make bounded reasoning more powerful.
Overall, these directions make the project even more compelling — they turn the nano model into a genuine cognitive-science testbed for both “understanding” and bounded rationality in artificial systems.

Discuss the relevance to our research questions and the interconnections among these concepts at a high level.These three concepts—reification in learning, Dehaene’s human number sense, and Simon’s scissors principle (with Gigerenzer’s ecological rationality extension)—form a tightly interconnected theoretical scaffold for our NanoGPT project. They are rarely discussed together in the LLM literature, yet in our controlled symbolic-algebra setting they illuminate exactly the same core questions: how implicit procedural competence becomes explicit conceptual understanding, how generalization and algebraic reasoning emerge, and what “understanding” actually looks like under strict computational bounds.
1. Reification as the bridge from procedural to conceptual knowledge
Reification (Sfard, Dubinsky’s APOS theory, Tall’s procept) is the process by which a learner turns an implicit procedure (e.g., fluidly rewriting 1+9 as 9+1 for ease of calculation) into an explicit object that can be named, reasoned about, and manipulated (e.g., recognizing and labeling “commutativity” as a general principle applicable to new operations). In our earlier discussion this was your first research question, and it sits at the heart of the conceptual–procedural knowledge interaction that almost no computational model has cleanly captured.
2. Dehaene’s number sense as the implicit foundation
Dehaene’s number sense (the Approximate Number System or ANS) is the innate, evolutionarily ancient, analog, magnitude-based representation of quantity that humans (and many animals) possess from infancy. It gives a fuzzy but rapid intuition for “more/less,” subitizing, and approximate arithmetic—yet it is non-symbolic and limited to rough estimates. Human mathematical development crucially involves reifying this implicit sense into exact symbolic systems (digits, operations, rules) via language and culture. Without a foundation like number sense, exact algebra remains brittle; with it, procedures become grounded in deeper relational understanding.
3. Simon’s scissors + Gigerenzer’s ecological rationality as the efficiency lens
Simon’s famous “scissors” metaphor captures bounded rationality: one blade is the structure of the task environment (in our case, the fully known, synthetic algebra grammar with its regularities of commutativity, inversion, canonical forms, etc.); the other blade is the agent’s limited computational resources (our tiny transformer’s fixed parameters, layers, and attention capacity). Rational behavior emerges when these two blades mesh—i.e., when the agent develops efficient representations and heuristics that exploit environmental structure rather than brute-force exhaustive search.
Gigerenzer and colleagues sharpened this into ecological rationality: “fast and frugal” heuristics are not cognitive flaws but smart adaptations precisely because they are tuned to the environment’s statistical regularities. In our project, the nano model’s strict bounds make this testable: success is not about unlimited optimization but about whether it discovers compact, reusable circuits (ecologically rational strategies) that let it “punch above its weight” on deeper or recombined expressions.
Interconnections and direct relevance to our research questions
These ideas converge powerfully in the nano setting because we control both blades of the scissors (the exact generative environment + the model’s tiny capacity) and can observe the reification process mechanistically.

Reification is the mechanism that turns number-sense-like implicit representations into ecologically rational tools. In the model we can first induce something analogous to Dehaene’s number sense (via curriculum that starts with simple magnitude patterns or distributed embeddings capturing approximate relations among digits). Progressive/curriculum training then gives the model a chance to reify those implicit patterns into explicit symbolic labels (e.g., “COMMUTE:” or “INVERT-MULTIPLY:”) or latent circuits. Success on your reification probes (meta-level queries about concepts, transfer to new operations) would be direct evidence that the model has moved from procedural fluency to conceptual objects—precisely the developmental leap Dehaene describes in humans.
Generalization and algebraic reasoning become tests of ecological rationality. Held-out digits, novel recombinations, or deeper nesting succeed only if the model has reified reusable abstractions (the “algebraic reasoning” question). This is bounded rationality in action: a resource-limited transformer that discovers environment-fitting shortcuts (e.g., a single attention head that propagates signs or inverses compositionally) will generalize far beyond its training distribution, mirroring how human number sense + reification enables us to handle novel arithmetic without exhaustive computation.
Curriculum order is the experimental lever on all three. Joint/random training may produce brittle, high-capacity procedural memorization (poor scissors fit). Progressive training—digits → signed → fractions/division-as-inverse → labeled rewrites—mimics the human trajectory from implicit number sense → symbolic reification → ecologically rational heuristics. We can measure whether this order yields (a) faster/better reification, (b) more robust generalization, and (c) more interpretable internal representations (the mechanistic signature of ecological rationality).
Misconceptions fit naturally as failed or partial reification. Biased training data can induce systematic errors (e.g., “always subtract smaller from larger”). The nano setup lets us ask whether these are mere procedural gaps or coherent (but incorrect) reified concepts—exactly the distinction that is hard to disentangle in humans but tractable here. Dehaene’s framework suggests such misconceptions often arise when the approximate number sense is not properly reified into exact symbolic relations; Simon/Gigerenzer would see them as heuristics that are ecologically rational in a restricted training environment but fail when the environment expands.

In short, our project operationalizes “understanding” as the emergence of ecologically rational, reified representations that are grounded in something like number-sense primitives and tuned to the algebra environment’s structure. The tiny transformer becomes a perfect laboratory: we can causally manipulate curriculum (the developmental path), induce or prevent reification (via name hints/labels), inspect the resulting internal circuits (mechanistic interpretability), and measure bounded-rational performance on generalization probes.
